Requirements Engineering Unit 2

The complete discipline of discovering, documenting, and validating what software must do — before a single line of code is written.

What is Requirements Engineering?

Definition: Requirements Engineering (RE) is the systematic process of eliciting, analyzing, specifying, validating, and managing the requirements of a software system.

RE bridges the gap between the problem world (customer needs) and the solution world (software to be built). It is the most critical phase of SDLC — errors here are the most expensive to fix later.

The Rule of 10: A defect found in requirements costs $1 to fix. Found in design: $10. In coding: $100. In testing: $1,000. In production: $10,000+. Getting requirements right is the highest ROI activity in software engineering.

Who are Stakeholders?

End users

People who will use the system day-to-day. Most important source of functional requirements.

Clients / customers

Funding the project. Define business goals and constraints. May differ from end users.

Domain experts

Know the problem domain (e.g., doctors in a hospital system). Provide tacit knowledge.

System engineers

Who will build it. Concerned with technical feasibility and platform constraints.

Regulators

Impose legal/compliance constraints. E.g., HIPAA for medical, PCI-DSS for payments.

Managers

Define budget, schedule, organizational priorities.

Stakeholder conflict: Different stakeholders will have conflicting requirements. A doctor wants maximum patient data access; a privacy regulator wants minimum data exposure. RE must surface and resolve these conflicts systematically — not ignore them.

Why RE is Hard — The Wicked Problem

  • Stakeholders don't know what they want until they see it (Prototyping solves this)
  • Tacit knowledge: Experts know things they cannot articulate ("I'll know it when I see it")
  • Changing requirements: Business context evolves; what's right today may be wrong in 6 months
  • Language ambiguity: Natural language is inherently imprecise ("The system should be fast")
  • Conflicting stakeholders: Power dynamics suppress minority voices
  • Incomplete domain knowledge: Analysts may not understand the problem domain deeply
Mnemonic — RE Process: "EASVM"
Elicitation → Analysis → Specification → Validation → Management

1. Functional Requirements (FR) What the system does

Definition: Statements that describe what the system should do — the specific behaviours, features, and functions the system must perform in response to inputs or events.

Written as: "The system shall [action] [object] [condition]."

Characteristics of Good FRs

  • Specific: One requirement per statement
  • Verifiable: Can be tested pass/fail
  • Unambiguous: Exactly one interpretation
  • Traceable: Linked to a stakeholder source

2. Non-Functional Requirements (NFR) Quality Attributes / "-ilities"

Definition: Constraints on the system's operation, behaviour, or development process. They describe how well the system performs its functions, not what it does.
Common exam trap: NFRs are NOT optional extras. They often determine whether a system is acceptable. A hospital system that is correct but crashes once a week is useless. NFRs are as important as FRs.

3. Domain Requirements Often Forgotten

Requirements derived from the application domain — reflect domain knowledge, laws, regulations, and industry standards. They often appear as constraints.

  • HMS: Patient data must be retained for at least 7 years (medical records law)
  • HMS: Prescription drugs can only be dispensed against a valid doctor's order
  • HMS: Controlled substances must have a separate audit log (regulatory)

MoSCoW Prioritisation

Must Have

Non-negotiable. System fails without it. E.g., patient registration, doctor authentication.

Should Have

Important but a workaround exists. E.g., automated appointment reminders.

Could Have

Nice to have, dropped if time is short. E.g., custom report builder for admin.

Won't Have (this time)

Explicitly out of scope for this release. E.g., AI-driven diagnosis suggestions.

2. Questionnaires (Surveys) Scalable

A set of written questions distributed to many stakeholders simultaneously. Used when: large user base, geographically distributed stakeholders, or quantitative data needed.

Types of Questions

  • Closed-ended: Likert scale, multiple choice. "How important is mobile access? (1–5)"
  • Open-ended: Free text. "What is the most painful part of the current process?"
  • Ranking: "Rank these features by importance."
Advantages
  • Scalable — reach 100s of users
  • Cost-effective
  • Anonymous responses → honest answers
  • Easy to aggregate and analyse
Disadvantages
  • Low response rates
  • Cannot probe or clarify
  • Misinterpretations go undetected
  • Design quality critically important

3. Observation (Ethnography) Discovers Tacit Knowledge

Analyst watches stakeholders perform their actual work — either silently (passive) or by participating (active/apprenticing). Reveals what users actually do vs. what they say they do.

Why observation is uniquely powerful: People often cannot articulate their own workflow — they have "muscle memory" knowledge. Watching them work uncovers requirements they would never think to mention in an interview. E.g., a nurse staples a handwritten note to a paper form because the system has no free-text field — a hidden requirement.
TypeDescriptionHMS Example
Passive observationAnalyst watches without interferingWatching a receptionist handle patient check-in
Active/participantAnalyst joins in the workAnalyst accompanies nurse on rounds, uses current system
Protocol analysisAsk user to think aloud while workingPharmacist narrates drug dispensing process aloud
Advantages
  • Reveals tacit and implicit requirements
  • Shows actual workflow vs stated workflow
  • Uncovers environmental constraints
  • Builds domain understanding
Disadvantages
  • Hawthorne effect — people change behaviour when watched
  • Time-intensive
  • Politically sensitive in some organizations
  • Observer may miss context without domain knowledge

4. Brainstorming Creative Discovery

A group session where participants generate ideas freely without judgment. Goal: maximum quantity of ideas in a short time; evaluation comes later.

Brainstorming Rules (Osborn's Rules)

  • No criticism: All ideas are welcome, no immediate judgment
  • Free-wheeling: Wild ideas are encouraged
  • Quantity over quality: More ideas = more seeds
  • Build on others: "Yes, and..." not "Yes, but..."

Structured Variants

Round-robin

Each person contributes one idea in turn. Prevents domination by vocal members.

6-3-5 method

6 people, 3 ideas each, 5 rounds of refinement. Generates 108 ideas in 30 minutes.

Brainwriting

Silent written version. Reduces groupthink and social inhibition.

5. Workshops (JAD / Focus Groups) Best for Conflict Resolution

Joint Application Development (JAD) — Structured workshops bringing all key stakeholders together to collaboratively define requirements, resolve conflicts, and reach consensus in a short, intensive session.

JAD Workshop Structure

1
Pre-workshop: Identify participants, define agenda, distribute pre-read materials (current system docs, problem statement)
2
Opening: Facilitator sets ground rules, defines objectives, introduces participants
3
Working sessions: Structured discussions by topic (patient flow, billing process, etc.). Use whiteboards, post-it notes, prototypes.
4
Conflict resolution: Facilitator manages disagreements, ensures minority voices heard, documents open issues
5
Closing: Review agreed requirements, assign action items, plan next steps
Advantages
  • Resolves conflicts directly between stakeholders
  • Faster than serial interviews
  • Creates shared understanding and ownership
  • Rich discussion reveals hidden assumptions
Disadvantages
  • Expensive to organise (all key people needed)
  • Dominated by senior or vocal participants
  • Requires skilled facilitator
  • Scheduling across departments is hard

Feasibility Study Pre-project Gate

Definition: A feasibility study is a preliminary investigation conducted before committing to a full project to determine whether the proposed software system is worth building and can be built within the given constraints.

The feasibility study answers one question: "Should we proceed?" It is the first gate in any serious software project.

1. Technical Feasibility Can we build it?

Assesses whether the required technology, tools, and expertise exist or can be acquired to build the system.

Questions to Answer

  • Does the technology to build the system exist?
  • Does the team have the required technical skills?
  • Can the system integrate with existing infrastructure?
  • Are there performance or scalability concerns that technology cannot meet?
  • Are the required hardware/software resources available?

2. Operational Feasibility Will it be used?

Assesses whether the system will be used effectively once deployed. Even technically perfect systems fail if users won't adopt them.

Factors to assess

  • User acceptance: Will end users embrace the system or resist it?
  • Workflow disruption: How much will daily work change? Will change management be needed?
  • Training: Can staff be trained within project constraints?
  • Organisational support: Does management champion the system?
  • Process compatibility: Does the system fit existing or planned processes?

HMS Operational Assessment

Doctors at City Hospital currently use paper-based records. Survey shows 65% resist digitisation. However, hospital administration mandates the system. Risk: low adoption without proper training and change management. Recommendation: phased rollout with intensive training programme and nurse/doctor champions.

3. Economic Feasibility (Cost-Benefit Analysis) Is it worth it?

Determines whether the financial benefits of the system justify its costs. The most decisive factor for business approval.

Cost Categories

Development costs

Analyst, developer, tester salaries. Hardware. Software licences. Training for development team.

Operational costs

Server hosting, maintenance, support staff, licence renewals, user training.

Opportunity costs

What is foregone by choosing this project over alternatives? What if money was invested differently?

Benefit Categories

Tangible benefits

Quantifiable in money. E.g., reduced paper costs, faster billing = earlier cash collection, fewer billing errors.

Intangible benefits

Hard to quantify but real. E.g., improved patient safety, better doctor satisfaction, enhanced hospital reputation.

4. Schedule Feasibility

Can the project be completed within the required timeframe? Considers team size, complexity, available technologies, and dependencies.

  • Is the deadline driven by business events (regulatory, competitive) or arbitrary?
  • Can scope be reduced if deadline cannot be moved?
  • Are there external dependencies (vendor APIs, hardware procurement) on the critical path?

5. Legal Feasibility

  • Does the system comply with HIPAA, GDPR, local health data laws?
  • Are there IP issues with software components being used?
  • Contractual obligations with existing vendors?

Requirements Analysis From Raw to Refined

Definition: Requirements analysis transforms raw, elicited requirements into a structured, complete, consistent, and unambiguous set that can be used for design. It bridges elicitation and specification.

Problems Found During Analysis

Incompleteness

Required behaviour in some situation is not described. "How should the system behave when the lab system is offline?"

Inconsistency

Two requirements contradict each other. FR-06: "All staff can view any patient." FR-12: "Only treating doctors can view patient records."

Ambiguity

"The system should be fast." Different stakeholders will interpret this differently.

Redundancy

Same requirement stated in two different places — may diverge over time if one is updated.

Infeasibility

A requirement that cannot be technically implemented within budget/time constraints.

Scope creep

Requirements gradually expand beyond the agreed scope, often by well-meaning additions.

Requirements Classification

During analysis, each requirement is classified by:

  • Type: FR, NFR, Domain Requirement
  • Priority: MoSCoW (Must / Should / Could / Won't)
  • Stability: Stable (unlikely to change) vs. Volatile (likely to change)
  • Source: Which stakeholder or document it came from (traceability)
  • Verification method: Test, inspection, demonstration, or analysis

Requirements Negotiation and Conflict Resolution

  • Identify conflicting requirements — surface the conflict explicitly
  • Understand the underlying needs — often conflicts are over solutions, not actual needs
  • Prioritise using business goals — which better serves the hospital's mission?
  • Compromise: Role-based access control can satisfy both security and utility stakeholders
  • Escalate if unresolvable — take to project sponsor for decision

Software Requirements Specification (SRS) IEEE 830 Standard

Definition: The SRS is a formal document that completely describes the behaviour of the software system to be developed. It is the contract between customers and developers — both parties sign it.

Why SRS is Critical

  • Communication: Single source of truth for all stakeholders
  • Legal contract: Defines what must be delivered
  • Design basis: Architects use it to create the system design
  • Test basis: QA derives all test cases from SRS
  • Change baseline: Any scope change requires SRS amendment (change control)
  • Maintenance guide: Future maintainers understand what and why

Components of the Analysis Model 4 Model Types

The analysis model is a set of structured diagrams and models created during requirements analysis to represent the system's requirements visually. It bridges requirements and design.

The 4 Analysis Model Components

Scenario-based models

Use cases, user stories. Model how actors interact with the system.

Data models

ERD, data dictionary. Model the structure and relationships of data.

Flow-oriented models

DFDs, sequence diagrams. Model how data flows and transforms.

Behavioural models

State diagrams, activity diagrams. Model system states and transitions.

1. Use Case Model Scenario-based

Shows what actors do with the system. A use case = one goal of one actor. Captures functional requirements as narratives.

Use Case Diagram Elements

  • Actor: External entity that interacts with the system (person, external system)
  • Use case: A function the system performs for an actor (oval)
  • Association: Actor participates in use case
  • «include»: Use case always includes another (mandatory sub-function)
  • «extend»: Use case optionally extends another (conditional add-on)
  • Generalisation: One actor/use case inherits from another

HMS Use Case: Patient Appointment Booking

Use Case ID:    UC-004
Use Case Name:  Book Appointment
Actors:         Receptionist (Primary), Doctor (secondary — calendar checked)
Preconditions:  Patient is registered in system; Receptionist is logged in
                with scheduling role; Doctor profile and availability is configured
Main Flow:
  1. Receptionist searches for patient by name or ID
  2. System displays patient profile and appointment history
  3. Receptionist selects "New Appointment"
  4. System displays available doctors by department and available time slots
  5. Receptionist selects doctor, date, and time slot
  6. System checks for doctor availability and patient conflict
  7. System confirms booking, generates Appointment ID
  8. System sends SMS/email confirmation to patient (includes: date, time, doctor, 
     department, location, instructions)
  9. System updates doctor's calendar

Alternate Flows:
  4a. No available slots for requested doctor:
      System suggests next available date or alternate doctor in same department.
  6a. Patient has existing appointment on same day:
      System warns receptionist; receptionist must confirm override or reschedule.
  8a. Patient contact details invalid:
      Notification fails silently; appointment is still booked; system logs failure.

Exception Flows:
  E1. System is unavailable (lab integration down):
      Appointment can still be booked; lab orders cannot be added until system recovers.

Postconditions:
  - Appointment record created with unique ID
  - Doctor's calendar updated
  - Patient notification sent
  - Appointment visible in receptionist's daily view

User Stories Agile Requirements

Definition: A user story is a short, informal description of a feature told from the perspective of the user or stakeholder. It is the primary requirements artifact in Agile development.

The 3-Part Template (Connextra Format)

As a [role], I want [feature/action] so that [benefit/goal].
  • Role: The type of user (not a specific person, but a user type/persona)
  • Feature: What the user wants to do — keep it action-oriented
  • Benefit: Why they want it — the business value. This is often omitted but crucial.

The 3 Cs of User Stories (Card, Conversation, Confirmation)

Card

The written story — brief enough to fit on a physical index card. It is a placeholder for a conversation, not the full specification.

Conversation

The discussion between developer and customer to elaborate details. Most information lives here, not on the card. Stories are conversation starters.

Confirmation

The acceptance criteria — testable conditions that confirm the story is done. Written before implementation (BDD).

Acceptance Criteria — GIVEN-WHEN-THEN

Format: GIVEN [context/precondition], WHEN [action/event], THEN [expected outcome].

HMS Example — US-01: Patient Registration

User Story: As a receptionist, I want to register a new patient so that they 
can receive hospital services.

Acceptance Criteria:

AC-1: Successful registration
  GIVEN a logged-in receptionist on the Patient Registration screen,
  WHEN they fill in Name, DOB, Gender, Phone, and Insurance ID and click "Register",
  THEN the system creates a unique PatientID (format: HMS-YYYY-NNNNN),
       displays a success confirmation, and prints a wristband label.

AC-2: Duplicate detection
  GIVEN an existing patient with Name="Ravi Kumar", DOB="12/05/1980",
  WHEN a receptionist tries to register another patient with the same Name and DOB,
  THEN the system displays a "Possible duplicate detected" warning with the existing
       patient's ID and asks to confirm or cancel before proceeding.

AC-3: Mandatory field validation
  GIVEN a registration form with the Phone field left empty,
  WHEN the receptionist clicks "Register",
  THEN the system highlights the Phone field in red with message 
       "Phone number is required" and does not create the patient record.

AC-4: Invalid date of birth
  GIVEN a DOB field with a future date entered,
  WHEN the receptionist clicks "Register",
  THEN the system shows error "Date of birth cannot be in the future."

Requirement Validation and Verification V&V

Validation: "Are we building the right system?" — Checking requirements against stakeholder needs.
Verification: "Are we building the system right?" — Checking requirements against standards and internal consistency.

Why Validate Requirements?

Because requirements can be wrong even after careful elicitation. Stakeholders may not have expressed their true needs, analysts may have misunderstood, or the business context may have changed. Validation catches errors before expensive design and coding begins.

Validation Techniques

1. Requirements Reviews (Inspections) Most Effective

A structured, systematic examination of the SRS by a team of reviewers. The most effective defect removal technique for requirements.

Fagan Inspection Process

1
Planning: Select team (author, moderator, reviewers, recorder). Distribute SRS. Set meeting time.
2
Overview: Author walks team through the requirements document at a high level.
3
Individual preparation: Each reviewer studies the document independently, noting defects.
4
Inspection meeting: Team reviews defects found. Moderator facilitates. Recorder logs all defects. No solutions — just find issues.
5
Rework: Author fixes all logged defects.
6
Follow-up: Moderator verifies all defects are fixed. If many defects remain, re-inspection is triggered.

Review Checklist (for HMS SRS)

  • Every FR has a unique ID, priority, and source?
  • Every FR is testable (can write pass/fail test)?
  • No ambiguous terms (fast, secure, easy, robust) used without metrics?
  • All actors in use cases are defined in Section 2.3?
  • All data elements in flows are in the data dictionary?
  • No two FRs contradict each other?
  • All NFRs have measurable metrics?
  • HIPAA compliance requirements addressed?

2. Prototyping for Validation

Build a quick mockup (UI prototype or throwaway system) and show it to stakeholders. Users react to something tangible — they validate by using it.

Why prototypes reveal hidden requirements: Users often cannot articulate abstract requirements, but they immediately recognize when something is wrong. "That's not how we do it" is more valuable feedback than any interview response.
  • Paper prototype: Hand-drawn screens. Fastest, cheapest.
  • Wireframe prototype: Digital mockup (Figma, Balsamiq). Realistic without code.
  • Throwaway prototype: Working code prototype to validate complex interactions. Discarded after validation.

3. Test Case Generation

Derive test cases from requirements. If a test case cannot be written, the requirement is not verifiable — it must be rewritten. This is a powerful validation technique.

Example — testing NFR-01 (Response <2 seconds): 
Test Case TC-PERF-001: Patient Search Performance
  Input: Search for "Ravi" with 450 concurrent simulated users
  Expected: 95% of responses return within 2 seconds
  Tool: Apache JMeter
  Pass criterion: 95th percentile latency ≤ 2000ms

4. Model Checking

Formal mathematical technique that checks properties of a model (DFD, state diagram) automatically. Used for safety-critical systems where formal proof is needed.

5. Automated Requirements Analysis Tools

  • Tools like IBM DOORS, Helix RM, Jama Connect manage requirements and check for consistency, completeness, and traceability automatically
  • NLP-based tools can detect ambiguous language ("should", "may", "fast") and flag for review

Common Requirements Pitfalls Edge Cases

  • Scope creep: Requirements grow unboundedly. Fix: strict change control; MoSCoW; frozen baseline
  • Gold plating: Developers add unrequested features. Fix: strict adherence to SRS; PO controls backlog
  • Analysis paralysis: Team spends so long on perfect requirements that project never starts. Fix: timeboxed analysis; "good enough" requirements with Agile iteration
  • Conflating requirements with solutions: "The system shall use MySQL" is a design decision in the SRS. Should be "The system shall support concurrent access by 500 users."
  • Ignoring NFRs: Teams focus on FR lists and defer NFRs. NFRs discovered late may require complete architecture rework.
  • Missing stakeholders: Night shift nurses in HMS were never consulted; system unusable for them.
  • Volatile requirements treated as stable: Insurance billing rules change every year — don't hardcode them; make them configurable.

Requirements Change Management

Once SRS is baselined (formally approved), changes follow a formal process:

1
Change Request (CR): Any stakeholder submits a formal CR describing the proposed change and business justification.
2
Impact Analysis: Analyst assesses impact on: other requirements, design, code, tests, timeline, budget.
3
Change Control Board (CCB): Group of stakeholders reviews CR and impact — approves, rejects, or defers.
4
Implementation: If approved, SRS is updated with version control. RTM updated. Design, code, tests updated.
5
Re-verification: Changed requirements re-reviewed and retested.
